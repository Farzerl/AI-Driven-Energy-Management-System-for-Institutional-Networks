# Business and Sustainability Model

## Primary user, payer, and beneficiary

- **Primary user:** Estates and Facilities personnel.
- **Customer or payer:** the institution operating the electrical network, supported where appropriate by innovation, research, or energy-efficiency funding.
- **Beneficiaries:** the institution, students, staff, and service users who benefit from improved visibility, reduced decision latency, and safer demand coordination.

## Value proposition

AI4I-EMS converts existing meter data into next-half-hour warnings, facility-level evidence, and operator-confirmed actions. It is intended to improve operational timing and accountability without requiring immediate replacement of the institution's entire electrical infrastructure.

A public-source planning model indicates:

- approximately 3.75 GWh over the eight-month study period;
- approximately USD 604,000 indicative expenditure using the public average tariff;
- approximately USD 14,000 under a central peak-management scenario.

These figures are not an invoice and are not realised savings. They establish the order of magnitude for a pilot business case.

## Delivery model

The initial route is institutional licensing and implementation support:

1. site and meter-interface assessment;
2. software configuration and data integration;
3. optional edge gateway and submeter deployment;
4. operator onboarding;
5. support, monitoring, and model review;
6. private tariff and measurement verification;
7. optional future supervised control for approved non-critical loads.

## Planning cost ranges

These figures are planning estimates, not supplier quotations.

| Level | Indicative setup | Indicative annual operation and support |
|---|---:|---:|
| Analytics and advisory deployment | USD 8,000 to 15,000 | USD 2,400 to 4,800 |
| Limited supervised pilot | USD 25,000 to 35,000 | USD 8,000 to 12,000 |
| Wider campus rollout | Up to USD 80,000 | USD 15,000 to 25,000 |

## Main cost drivers

- meter and communications integration;
- edge gateway and optional submeters;
- installation, protection, and commissioning;
- institutional server or approved hosting;
- data engineering and model maintenance;
- user onboarding, support, and monitoring;
- future control hardware and safety testing.

## Sustainability route

The product can be sustained through an institutional licence, implementation fee, and annual support agreement. A public institution may combine internal funding with innovation or energy-efficiency grants during the pilot.

## First pilot market

The University of Zimbabwe is the first realistic pilot environment because it provides the institutional context, historical meter evidence, and departmental technical oversight. Expansion should follow only after reliable ingestion, user acceptance, and verified measurement of operational outcomes.

## Adoption risks

- incompatible or inaccessible meter interfaces;
- limited flexible load;
- procurement delays;
- unclear ownership after the demonstration;
- excessive alert burden;
- public planning tariff differing from the account tariff;
- safety concerns around future control;
- insufficient maintenance funding.

## Pilot success measures

- at least 95% data availability;
- model performance retained relative to persistence;
- controlled alert burden;
- documented operator response;
- measurable peak-demand or peak-energy change;
- private tariff verification;
- no unauthorised switching;
- no critical-load control events;
- support owner and scale decision identified.
